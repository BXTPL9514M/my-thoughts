The Comfort of Judging Others

Ever wondered how we decide our worth among people—or among crowds? Most of the time, our thoughts are not really our own. They are shaped by how others react to us. We become so focused on being watched that we slowly lose control over how we behave in front of people.

In trying to fit in, we forget who we actually are. Instead of understanding ourselves, we become interested in judging others. We observe how people behave toward us or toward someone else, and we silently decide who deserves respect and who does not.

People judge because others are different. And when someone cannot be understood or controlled, the easiest response is disrespect. Sometimes it appears as frustration, sometimes as silence, and sometimes as anger that is carried home and released onto the people closest to us.

These are the people who love us, who respect us, and who rarely speak against us. Yet they become the ones who suffer most. We hurt them because they feel safe to hurt.

Now imagine being on the other side. Imagine being the person who is judged, disrespected, or emotionally unloaded on for no real reason. How would that feel? Not fair. Not justified. Not deserved.

Yet this is human nature. There is a strange comfort in watching others suffer—it feels distant, almost entertaining. But the moment that suffering turns toward us, it suddenly becomes cruelty.

Maybe growth begins when we stop enjoying judgment and start questioning why we need it at all.

— Mohammad Laxmidhar
